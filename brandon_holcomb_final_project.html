Price's Law applied to Movie Revenues¶

Price's law states that square root of the total number of producers will produce at about of a given output.

This will be an investigation as to wheether Price's law apllies to a single year (2015 and 2010) and to the total number of movies over all the years provided.

After sorting the data by revenue in descending order there seems to be an issue with the data.

For Example The Net(1995) is listed as being in the top ten grossing films all time when adjusted for revenue.

However it appears there was an error when entering the data in. The number should be 110,627,965 (according to IMDB) and not 1,106,279,658. It appears there was an errant '8' added to the figure for some reason.

Also it seems that revenue is not recorded for all films. So for this reason they should be removed from the data set as revenue is the chief factor we will be looking at.

Just eyeballing it, it looks like the sample size will still be large enough to get a good indication of whether Price's law seems to apply.

Conclusion¶

Price's law seems to apply to movie revenues if adjusted by year but not when all the movies for all years were taken into account.

It appears that the provided data has issues with all numbers related to revenues not being provided and potentially not being accurate.

Also, it may be more helpful to check this by whether or nor a movie had a theater release as some of the entires in the data appear to have been TV releases. Natually revenue figures for TV realeases will be different and possibly more difficult to collect than Movies release revenues.

While there seem to be issues with the data I think we can conclude that nothing found will prove Price's Law doesn't apply to movie revenues. And we might reasonably epect to see a relatively small group of those movies, whether its the top 20 percent or the top 1 percent, be disproprtionatly productive compared to the rest of the population.
